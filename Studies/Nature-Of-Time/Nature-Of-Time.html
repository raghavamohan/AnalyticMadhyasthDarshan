Nature of Time

Author: AnalyticMadhyasthDarshan.org — a group of people studying philosophy. Source repository: raghavamohan/AnalyticMadhyasthDarshan.

Edited on: June 28, 2026, 7:49 PM IST

The question: What is the nature of time ()? How does relate timeless Omnipresence () to the duration of unit-activity, numerical reckoning, and the past–present–future structure — and how does that compare with Advaita Vedanta, philosophy of time, and modern physics (spacetime, cosmology, entropy)?

This study develops the comparative treatment begun in The Ontology of Coexistence (§1.4, §2.7, §4.3). That paper states ’s core claim — as duration of activity, timeless , numerical reckoning within existence — and defers past/present/future structure, JVD on “activity eternally present,” Advaita trikaal language, and systematic comparison with McTaggart, eternalism/presentism, and spacetime physics to this study (Ontology §6.2.9).

Standpoint and scope

These studies are written from the standpoint of a scientist and technologist — someone trained to graduate-level physics and mathematics, at home with contemporary cosmology, quantum theory, conservation laws, and the logic of formal models.

From that background, a familiar picture of nature is hard to avoid: consciousness appears as something the brain does — an epiphenomenon, functional outcome, or emergent property of particular configurations of very large numbers of physical particles. Modern physics and cognitive science are powerful on mechanism, prediction, and public evidence; yet the hard problem of consciousness, the status of the self, and the reality of value remain fiercely contested. The standpoint taken here does not treat those gaps as settled in favour of matter-only reductionism.

The work begins where that scientific picture leaves open questions — and asks whether offers a coherent alternative worth examining seriously. This paper reads the primary texts carefully, states what follows from the darshan itself, and compares it in parallel with what we know from physics and the natural sciences, Advaita Vedanta, and modern Western philosophy (especially philosophy of time). Physics and mathematics are one leg of that comparison, not the only one. The aim is rigorous comparative understanding — testing definitions, internal consistency, and fit with public knowledge — not persuasion or devotional endorsement.

These topical studies state the philosophy in clear, checkable prose first. A separate formal mathematical treatment may follow once the definitions and relations are stable across the studies; this paper does not assume or require that treatment.

Terms are defined in the essay where they first arise; a consolidated glossary keyed to §§1–3 appears in the Appendix.

1. The Madhyasth Darshan Answer

holds that time () belongs to the activity of units, not to Omnipresence as ground. To understand that claim, we must look past two common assumptions: the Newtonian view that time is an absolute cosmic container flowing independently of events, and the Advaitic view that time is an ultimate illusion (). The darshan grounds time in the objective reality of nature (), defining it through the ongoing activity () of units ().

1.1 Time as the duration of activity

does not posit time as an independent dimension, substance, or background framework. It derives time directly from the motion and transformation of units:

“Time (): Duration of an activity is referred to as time.” - MVD, p. 34

Because every physical (jada) and sentient (chaitanya) unit in existence is eternally active through the single triad (effort–motion–result) — in sentient read toward completeness through projection, reflection, and (The Ontology of Coexistence §1.4) — activity is perpetual. Time is the inseparable duration of this activity. It is not something units “move through”; it is a property of their motion and state.

“Time is measured by the duration of an activity.” - SB, p. 65

Where there is no transforming activity, there is no time in this sense. That restriction applies directly to Omnipresence ().

1.2 Kaal, rachna, and vistar — a duration taxonomy

does not treat “duration” as one undifferentiated category. MVD groups three related terms:

“Time (): Duration of an activity is referred to as time.” “Composition (rachna): The bounds of matter is referred to as composition or object, which is measured.” “Expanse (vistar): The span of a composition is referred to as expanse.” - MVD, p. 196

belongs to activity — the ongoing of units. Rachna and vistar belong to bounded matter: how long a composition lasts as a counted object and how far that composition extends. MVD adds that the duration of matter’s existential measure and form depends on its organisation and state (MVD, p. 41) — not on a freestanding temporal container.

Absolute energy ( as uniform energy) is present in every time, place, and object; no place or object lacks it. Places devoid of matter are acknowledged, but no time devoid of that energy is proven (MVD, p. 41). in the strict sense still applies only where activity occurs; matter-duration (rachna, vistar) tracks bounded configurations, not the ground.

1.3 The timelessness of Omnipresence (satta)

Existence () is the inseparable coexistence of units and Omnipresence. While units are formful and ceaselessly active, is formless, all-pervasive, and entirely non-transforming (-shunya).

Because performs no actions and undergoes no change, duration in the sense of does not apply to it. MVD describes Omnipresence as existing “at all places and times, and … eternally present” (MVD, p. 34). That “all times” language names omnipresence, not temporal becoming in : there is no place or time where formless existence is absent (SB, p. 69). Omnipotence is present everywhere and at all times, and all unit-activities are encompassed within it — indivisible from it as regulation and supreme cause (mahakaran), not as efficient trigger of particular change (MVD, p. 296; The Ontology of Coexistence §1.4).

At the level of units (), time is real as the duration of . At the level of the ground (), there is absolute timelessness as -shunya stasis. The two levels are not contradictory because is defined only through , not as a freestanding dimension in which sits.

MVD uses in a second, contextual sense for awakened humans: omnipresent appears as law at the time of vocation, resolution at the time of thinking, bliss at the time of realisation, and justice at the time of behaviour (MVD, p. 34). These are modalities of human activity, not independent cosmic time — the same ground expressed in distinct phases of work, thought, experience, and conduct.

1.4 Trikaalabadh, atma, and timeless coexistence

SB states that the harmony of consciousness and matter is:

“…a timeless truth valid across all three times.” - SB, p. 48

The Sanskrit trikaalabadh (binding across past, present, and future) applies to coexistence as a whole — not to prove that unit-activity is illusory, but to show that what is understood about coexistence does not come and go with a moment. Existence does not begin and does not end; is atemporal as ground, yet coexistence is valid across the three times. as duration belongs to the activity of units, not to as efficient cause (The Ontology of Coexistence §1.4).

MVD adds a sentient-level three-time claim distinct from SB’s binding of coexistence:

“Atma exists in the form of mediative activity across all three times. Existence is unchanging; its realisation happens in atma, and enlightenment happens in .” - MVD, p. 299

Atma is mediative activity — free from generative and degenerative pressure (MVD, p. 296). Its presence across the three times names the unchanging existential element in sentient nature, realised in atma and enlightened in , while unit-activity and as duration continue in transforming nature. SB’s trikaalabadh binds coexistence; MVD’s formula binds atma as the mediative pole through which existence is experienced as unchanging. Both affirm three-time validity without sublating the world.

This is ’s answer to a question Advaita also faces with trikaal language: how can the absolute be related to past, present, and future without being swallowed by temporal becoming? ’s reply is bifurcated ontology — timeless ground, temporally active units, coexistence and atma binding truth across the three times without sublating the world.

1.5 Relative truth, measurement, and numerical reckoning

SB and MVD sort truth into three kinds. Through awakening, one recognises absolute truth as nature saturated in Omnipotence, relative truth as direction, time, and place, and objective truth as form, properties, essential nature, and (SB, p. 65). enters as relative truth — alongside direction and place, not as absolute or objective truth in its own right:

“Relative truth is evident in the form of direction, time, and place. … The duration of activity, as time, is a humanly devised numerical reckoning within existence.” - SB, p. 251

MVD defines (truth) as uniformly perceivable, existent, and realisable at all three times — the eternal presence (nitya vartaman) of absolute, relative, and objective truth in existence (MVD, p. 336). Time as relative truth is therefore real within coexistence — humans measure and coordinate activity-duration — but its numerical form is conventional:

“The measurement of time, expanse and different compositions is through mathematics.” - MVD, p. 195

JVD adds that every duration appears in numerical form and that purposes can be recognised on the basis of time or activity (JVD, p. 85). Science, in JVD’s public-discourse vocabulary, offers a mechanism to associate time with activity once comprehensive object-knowledge is established through analysis (JVD, pp. 191–192). Time-reckoning compares one activity against a standard reference activity (such as the Earth’s rotation). The underlying duration is real; quantification and coordinate systems belong to human epistemology within the human order (), not to the fundamental ontology of .

1.6 Activity eternally present and the three times

emphasises the present as the locus of causal reality. JVD states the central passage deferred from The Ontology of Coexistence §6.2.9:

“Every numerical moment of time, which is taken to be past or future, is found to be present in the form of activity. Thus, it becomes studiable that activity is eternally present.” - JVD, p. 85

Read carefully, this is not a denial that humans experience past and future. We remember, anticipate, and plan; numerical reckoning distinguishes earlier and later durations. The claim is ontological and pedagogical: whatever moment we label past or future, studiable reality appears as present activity. In insentient nature, anticipated change appears as the projected result (parinam) of current activity; in sentient , the same triad continues but toward (resolution) through projection and reflection rather than physicochemical parinam (The Ontology of Coexistence §1.4). Only present activity possesses causal efficacy in the darshan’s order of nature.

Where does the “past” survive if it is not an independent temporal place? In it persists in present forms at two levels. Physically, as inherited structural traces and configurations within material units (jada ) — consistent with conservation through transformation, not annihilation (The Ontology of Coexistence §1.12). In the human order, through the faculties of : (sahaj chitran — natural visualisation) includes time () among its eight reckoning activities — form, property, count, time, expanse, effort, motion, and result (MVD, p. 327); ’s innateness is enlightenment of time, activity, and truth (MVD, p. 204); carries acceptances and knowledge forward across cycles (MVD, p. 90; The Ontology of Coexistence §1.9). Through and , past and future become numerically distinguishable categories for humans — not because duration ceases for insentient units, which have as activity-duration without , but because human study compares visualised durations against present activity (JVD, p. 85). The full map of , , , , and atma belongs in the ongoing study Philosophy-Of-Mind-And-; this paper notes only what time requires from that structure.

“Activity eternally present” therefore names two related truths: (1) units never cease their ; (2) the human study of time, activity, and present becomes coherent when we stop treating past and future as independent places and instead trace them to ongoing activity now. This is closer to present-centred process ontology than to eternalism, but it is not identical to Western presentism, because the past is real as completed activity (trace, configuration, and human reckoning through , , and ) rather than as sheer non-being.

1.7 Place, time, and eternal presence

Questions that treat place and time as containers into which existence must be fitted — for example, where the four orders “were” before Earth — rely on constructs that SB rejects at the level of existential grandeur. In the intrinsic magnificence of existence, characterised by eternal presence, place and time do not interfere (SB, p. 103). Existence is not limited to place or time; its magnificence is beyond those constraints, even though humans express reality through words and imagine places and times speculatively (SB, p. 138).

That claim complements, rather than contradicts, the reality of as activity-duration and of relative truth as direction, time, and place. At the unit level, direction, place, and measured duration are operationally valid — mutual reference points among , numerical reckoning of activity (SB, p. 65). At the existential level, is eternally present (nitya vartaman): what was, continues to be; what was not, does not happen (SB, p. 103). Place and time as human reckoning frameworks coordinate activity within coexistence; they do not bound or originate existence itself.

1.8 The primacy of the present

Because units are always in now, the present is the actualised state of existence. refuses both the Advaitic sublation of temporal becoming at the highest truth and the block-universe picture on which all moments are equally real and static. Change is ontologically real; the present is where transformation occurs. Numerical time is how humans coordinate that reality across scales — from atomic cycles to cosmic epochs — without reifying time as a container independent of activity.

2. The Advaita Vedanta Answer

Advaita Vedanta holds that existence in the absolute sense () is alone — beginningless, changeless, partless, and entirely outside temporal becoming (VC, v. 393).

2.1 Time at vyavahara and paramartha

The Upanishads place the absolute both within and beyond temporal structure. The Mandukya Upanishad opens by defining the ultimate reality (represented by Om) as encompassing all temporal phases and transcending them:

“All this is the letter Om. A clear explanation of it is this: All that is past, present, and future is verily Om. And whatever else is beyond the three periods of time is also verily Om.” - MU, v. 1

Time () is a structural feature of the empirical world (). At this level, past, present, and future, causal succession, and birth and death are operationally valid. Time governs the physical universe and is wielded by Ishvara (Saguna ) as cosmic force of transformation and dissolution:

“I am the world-destroying Time, grown in stature…” - BG 11.32

At , however, the entire universe of names and forms (nama-roopa) is — dependent appearance projected by Maya. Empirical time is sublated upon realisation of the timeless Self ():

“There is neither death nor birth, neither a bound nor a struggling soul, neither a seeker after Liberation nor a liberated one – this is the ultimate truth.” - VC, v. 574

2.2 Trikaal language and the contrast with trikaalabadh

Advaita’s trikaal formulas (as in MU, v. 1) serve a non-dual purpose: is not confined to one temporal phase because is the truth of all phases and also beyond them. Temporal succession belongs to ; at liberation, the Self is discovered as never truly bound by birth, death, or succession (The Ontology of Coexistence §2.7).

uses parallel trikaal vocabulary with a different ontological commitment. SB’s trikaalabadh binds coexistence across the three times while affirming perpetual unit-activity and world-realness (jagat satat). Advaita validates time operationally but finally sublates it; validates time as real duration of real activity and denies that realization cancels the world. The dispute is not over whether humans live in past–present–future structure, but over whether temporal becoming has final ontological weight.

3. Modern Physics and Philosophy of Time

Mainstream science answers questions about time through relativity, thermodynamics, and cosmology — not through activity-based ontology. Those models nonetheless form the main comparative partners for ’s .

3.1 Spacetime and the block universe

In Newtonian mechanics, time was an absolute, uniform background. Einstein’s Special and General Relativity showed that time is relative to the observer’s frame and inextricably woven with space into a four-dimensional manifold: spacetime. In General Relativity, spacetime is dynamical — it can expand, curve, and in some models exhibit local beginnings (The Ontology of Coexistence §4.3).

A common philosophical interpretation is the block universe: past, present, and future coexist statically as coordinates in a four-dimensional block. The flow of time is treated as a feature of consciousness or description, not as an objective passage; there is no privileged “now” in the block itself.

3.2 McTaggart, A-theory, B-theory, eternalism, and presentism

J.M.E. McTaggart’s 1908 analysis distinguishes an A-series (events as past, present, or future) from a B-series (events as earlier or later than one another). A-theories — including presentism and some growing-block models — treat the present (or the present’s passage) as ontologically special. B-theories — including eternalism and many block-universe readings — treat temporal order as relations among equally real events without a moving “now.”

’s present-centred activity ontology is A-theoretic in spirit: the present is where actualises. It is not, however, a standard presentism, because past activity leaves real traces (configuration, record, and — in humans — / reckoning) and future states are real anticipations rooted in present activity rather than empty non-entities. Eternalism and the block universe are the clearest rivals: they treat temporal passage as derivative or illusory, whereas treats ongoing activity as the bedrock of .

3.3 Relational time

Relational theories (Leibniz, Mach, and contemporary physicists such as Carlo Rovelli) deny that time exists as an independent container. Time is the network of changes and relations among physical systems; we measure it by comparing one variable’s change to another (clock hands relative to the sun, atomic transitions relative to laboratory standards).

’s definition of as duration of activity is structurally close to relational time: no freestanding temporal substance, only durations of transforming units. The difference is ontological scope — includes sentient unit-activity and ties duration to across orders of nature, not only to physical observables in spacetime.

Whitehead’s process philosophy shares that relational realism and treats time as internal to becoming. His basic units are “actual entities” — “drops of experience, complex and interdependent” (Whitehead 1929, p. 28). Process metaphysics is a partial affinity with activity-based : change and duration belong to unit-becoming, not to an absolute container. The contrast remains persistence: Whitehead’s occasions are momentary and “perpetually perish” subjectively, gaining “objective immortality” only as data for successors (Whitehead 1929, p. 44), whereas ’s is a persisting immortal individual and insentient units retain configuration through transformation rather than subjective perishing (The Ontology of Coexistence §3.6.4).

3.4 Cosmological time and beginningless existence

Cosmology asks whether the physical universe has a beginning. The standard ΛCDM model implies a hot big bang roughly 13.8 billion years ago; research programs in loop quantum cosmology, conformal cyclic cosmology, and eternal inflation explore whether a beginningless substrate may underlie local cosmic epochs (The Ontology of Coexistence §4.2).

holds that existence () is beginningless and endless, while configurations of units undergo ceaseless transformation. That claim is conceptually resonant with beginningless cosmological models and with conservation-based physics, but it is not established by them. The darshan’s further claims — immortal , order-specific cycles, activity eternally present — remain metaphysical assertions distinct from current cosmological evidence.

3.5 Entropy and the arrow of time

Thermodynamics explains the macroscopic arrow of time — why eggs break but un-break, and why we remember the past rather than the future — through increasing entropy in closed systems (Second Law). Memory and causation align with the entropy gradient in the human branch of physical history.

does not derive the arrow of time from entropy; its present-centrism is compatible with thermodynamic asymmetry rather than competing with it. Within the darshan, temporal direction comes from the ontology of activity itself — but in modes that must not be conflated. Insentient units change through (effort–motion–result): the three are inseparable joint forms of the same activity, and insentient exchange also exhibits cyclical restoration and give–take complementarity (SB, pp. 52–53, 58; The Ontology of Coexistence §1.4). Sentient uses the same triad, read toward constitutional, activity, and conduct completeness through projection, reflection, and — qualitative change without physicochemical parinam (SB, p. 55). Development (vikas) across the four orders — material, bio, animal, and knowledge — adds a further directional structure: a seed developing into a tree illustrates real order-specific transformation, not mere statistical fluctuation.

The darshan therefore offers a partial ontological orientation toward temporal asymmetry — present-centred , directional vikas, and (in humans) -mediated carry-forward — without replacing thermodynamics. Whether , order-specific vikas, or sentient could formally ground or constrain entropy asymmetry remains an open question (§6.4). What adds in any case is an account of what time is (duration of activity) and where becoming is real (unit now).

4. Comparative Summary

The three frameworks agree that humans live with past–present–future structure and measurable duration. They disagree over what time is, whether the ground of reality is timeless, and whether temporal becoming is finally real.

5. Critical Review

5.1 Madhyasth Darshan — activity-based time, present-centred realism

Strengths. The definition of through is internally consistent with the darshan’s coexistential ontology: timeless , active , no freestanding temporal container. The three-truth framework situates as relative truth alongside direction and place while preserving real activity-duration. Trikaalabadh on coexistence and atma’s mediative presence across the three times answer part of the Advaita challenge without sublating the world. JVD’s “activity eternally present” gives a distinctive present-centred account that resists both block eternalism and world-denying non-dualism. Numerical reckoning as convention aligns measurement with human epistemology while preserving real duration.

Weaknesses. The relation between “every numerical moment is present as activity” and ordinary memory, anticipation, and historical record needs tighter formalisation — especially versus eternalism and versus Advaita’s turiya witness of temporal states (The Ontology of Coexistence §2.2). The darshan suggests directional structure through vikas and the activity triads but does not derive thermodynamic asymmetry from them or fully reconcile activity-based with relativistic spacetime and cosmological horizons. Public evidence standards remain those of metaphysics, not laboratory physics.

offers a coherent activity-based temporal ontology grounded in its texts, but its strongest claims about present-centred causation and beginningless coexistence remain to be tested against rival philosophies of time and against physical theory in detail.

5.2 Advaita Vedanta — timeless absolute, robust empirics

Strengths. Advaita integrates trikaal language with a rigorous distinction between empirical order and absolute truth. Time is not ignored: it governs , ethics, and cosmic dissolution (BG 11.32). The Mandukya analysis of waking, dream, and deep sleep offers a first-person route into how temporal states appear and are witnessed.

Weaknesses. From the Madhyasth perspective, sublating time at makes it harder to give final ontological weight to relationships, nature, and conduct — even though is law-governed and ethically binding (The Ontology of Coexistence §6.3). Advaita’s reply that ethics deepens at realization is substantial; the dispute is over whether world-realness at every order matches ’s standard.

Advaita is strongest where it treats temporal experience as a ladder toward timeless witness-consciousness; it is weaker as a realist ontology of perpetual unit-activity and coexistence across the three times.

5.3 Modern physics and philosophy of time — precision without agreement

Strengths. Relativity and thermodynamics supply precise, testable accounts of intervals, horizons, and entropy gradients. Relational and process philosophies share structural affinities with activity-based duration. McTaggart’s A/B distinction clarifies where sits among presentism, eternalism, and block models.

Weaknesses. Physics does not settle whether the block universe, presentism, or a relational variant is true — interpretations of quantum gravity and cosmology remain contested. Relativity’s spacetime is not the same object as ’s ; mapping one to the other risks category error. Entropy explains asymmetry but not the metaphysical status of “now” or of unit-activity.

Modern science is indispensable for measurement and dynamical law; it does not by itself answer what time is in the Madhyasth or Advaita sense, nor does it validate immortal or timeless Omnipresence.

6. Open Problems

  1. The mechanisms of the past in and jada. While the past survives physically in jada configurations and, in humans, is reckoned through (MVD, p. 327) and carried forward through , formalising how accesses physical traces without dualism requires deeper work — to be developed alongside Philosophy-Of-Mind-And- — particularly when comparing against block-universe models that treat the past as statically real rather than dependent.

  2. Trikaalabadh, atma, and Advaita trikaal. Both traditions use three-time language. SB’s trikaalabadh binds coexistence; MVD’s atma is mediative activity across the three times (p. 299). A fuller treatment must state whether these claims are logically independent of Advaita’s sublation, or whether the vocabularies only appear parallel in English.

  3. Relativity and activity-based . If is duration of unit-activity and numerical reckoning is conventional, how should one read coordinate time in General Relativity, horizon effects, and the block-universe interpretation? Is spacetime a useful human reckoning on large-scale activity, or a rival ontology?

  4. Entropy and the activity triad. While directional vikas across orders and the single triad with its sentient unfolding offer partial orientation toward temporal asymmetry compatible with thermodynamics, it remains unsettled whether this structure could formally ground or mathematically constrain entropy asymmetry in physical systems, or whether mapping it directly is a category mistake.

  5. Cosmological beginning and beginningless existence. Resonance between beginningless coexistence and some cosmological models is conceptual, not evidential. What would distinguish ’s claim from eternal inflation or cyclic cosmology on public grounds?

  6. Evidence and operational criteria. Duration of activity is objective within the darshan, but constitutional completeness and -mediated activity are not presently measurable in physics. Until operational criteria exist, the temporal ontology stands as metaphysics compatible with conservation principles, not as established science.

  7. Immortal and the experience of . Because undergoes qualitative change through (resolution) rather than physicochemical parinam, it is constitutionally complete (gathanpurna) and immortal (The Ontology of Coexistence §§1.9–1.10). still applies to as duration of activity — the question is not whether an immortal unit has time, but how the qualitative experience of duration for differs from insentient units undergoing and from the decaying body (shareer) whose configurations transform through roopantaran rather than annihilation. Exploring that difference remains a future area of inquiry alongside Philosophy-Of-Mind-And-.

Appendix: Quick Glossary

Key terms from §§1–3 are collected here for quick reference. Each term is also defined where it first appears in the exposition.

Terms in §1

Term Plain meaning
Time (kaal) The duration of unit-activity (kriya). Not an independent cosmic dimension or container.
Composition (rachna) The bounds or duration of matter as a measured object — distinct from kaal as activity-duration (MVD, p. 196).
Expanse (vistar) The span of a composition — duration at the level of bounded matter, not freestanding space-time (MVD, p. 196).
Relative truth One of three truth kinds in SB/MVD: direction, time, and place — with kaal as human numerical reckoning of activity-duration (SB, pp. 65, 251).

Unit (ikai) A countable, bounded entity in nature. All units are active; hence duration applies to them.

Terms in §3
